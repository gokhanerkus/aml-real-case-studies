# Case 00 — Risk Assessment

## Objective

This section converts the analytical findings into a structured financial crime risk assessment. The goal is to show how the reported trade pattern would be assessed under an AML/CFT framework.

---

## Risk Summary

| Risk Dimension | Assessment | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Jurisdiction risk | High | The trade occurs in a conflict-affected corridor with degraded border control and weak traceability |
| Sanctions risk | High | Exposure to ISIS-influenced territory creates concern over direct or indirect dealings with designated actors |
| Terrorist financing risk | High | Trade flows in a terrorist-controlled environment may generate fees, tolls, diversion, or facilitation payments |
| TBML risk | High | Trade expansion in a conflict setting is consistent with trade-based value transfer typologies |
| Counterparty risk | High | Beneficial ownership and true end-user visibility are likely reduced |
| Documentation risk | High | Customs, shipping, and invoicing records may be incomplete, distorted, or difficult to verify |
| Overall risk | High | The combined risk factors justify a conservative control posture and enhanced scrutiny |

### Placement of this case
This case sits in the **High Likelihood / High Impact** zone because:
- the environment is structurally high risk
- the trade pattern is abnormal relative to expected conflict behaviour
- the potential consequences include sanctions breaches, terrorist-financing exposure, and illicit value transfer

---

## Key Risk Drivers

### 1. Conflict-zone trade
Trade continued in a zone affected by active conflict and reduced state control.

This matters because conflict zones typically weaken:
- customs oversight
- transport verification
- counterparty due diligence
- reliable payment tracing

### 2. Abnormal trade growth
Reported exports increased rather than declining after territorial control shifted.

This is a red flag because it suggests the trade relationship may have adapted to the conflict through:
- informal channels
- brokers
- route changes
- undisclosed counterparties

### 3. Terrorist-control exposure
The presence of ISIS-influenced territory introduces a direct terrorist-financing concern.

Even if the trade was partly legitimate, the environment creates exposure to:
- taxation by armed groups
- coercive payments
- diversion of goods
- facilitation by linked actors

### 4. Reduced transparency
Conflict conditions make it harder to verify:
- who the end user is
- whether the goods match the declaration
- whether the route was genuine
- whether the payment chain is complete

---

## Scenario-Based Risk Outcome

| Scenario | Risk View | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Direct trade with a verified legitimate counterparty | Still elevated | Conflict environment alone creates residual risk |
| Trade through intermediaries | Higher risk | Intermediary layers can obscure the true beneficiary |
| Trade with weak or missing documentation | Very high risk | Documentation gaps weaken the ability to validate legitimacy |
| Any link to designated or sanctioned actors | Critical risk | Would require immediate escalation and restrictive action |

---

## Control Implications

| Control Area | Expected Action |
|---|---|
| Screening | Screen all parties, vessels, brokers, and transport links against sanctions and adverse media sources |
| EDD | Apply enhanced due diligence to counterparties and trade flows |
| Trade validation | Check invoices, customs forms, shipping documents, and payment records for consistency |
| Ownership review | Identify beneficial owners and control persons |
| Route review | Assess whether the trade route is economically and logistically credible |
| Escalation | Escalate to MLRO or equivalent function if any link to designated actors is identified |

---

## Final Risk Decision

**Overall assessment: High risk**

### Why
The case combines:
- conflict exposure
- border control degradation
- trade growth under abnormal conditions
- potential terrorist-financing relevance
- opacity over counterparties and documentation

### Practical implication
This would not be handled as a routine trade relationship. It would require:
- enhanced scrutiny
- additional source verification
- careful documentation review
- escalation if any designated-party linkage is found

---

## Analyst Note

The key lesson from this case is that trade growth is not automatically a positive signal. In high-risk environments, expanding trade can be a warning sign rather than evidence of normal commercial activity.
