# Case 00 — Cross-Border Trade Risk (ISIS-linked Network)

This case is based on original investigative reporting later cited in academic counterterrorism literature.

---

## 1. Context

In 2013, control of the Kilis–Öncüpınar border region weakened significantly, allowing non-state armed groups to influence cross-border trade flows between Turkey and Syria.

Following this shift, a sharp increase in exports to Syria was observed.

---

## 2. Key Risk Indicators

- Sudden spike in cross-border trade volume  
- Trade activity concentrated in conflict-affected regions  
- Loss of formal state control over border checkpoints  
- Presence of non-state armed actors controlling logistics routes  
- Use of informal or unregulated trade channels  

---

## 3. Risk Assessment

The environment presents a **high risk of trade-based money laundering (TBML)** and potential terrorist financing.

Key concerns include:

- Goods and funds moving through partially controlled territories  
- Difficulty verifying end-users and counterparties  
- Blending of legitimate and illicit trade flows  
- Weak enforcement of customs and regulatory oversight  

---

## 4. Analytical Insight

This case illustrates how:

- Regulatory gaps + geopolitical instability  
→ create conditions for illicit financial flows  

- Trade data anomalies  
→ can act as early warning indicators of financial crime  

Such patterns are highly relevant for AML transaction monitoring and onboarding risk assessment.

---

## 5. AML Relevance

From a compliance perspective, this scenario would trigger:

- Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)  
- Geographic risk flagging  
- Transaction monitoring alerts for abnormal trade patterns  
- Investigation into beneficial ownership and counterparties

It highlights the importance of combining OSINT, trade data analysis, and risk-based compliance frameworks in identifying financial crime risks.
